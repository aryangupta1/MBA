01 — Understanding the spectrum

Primary Remote Models

Think of these models as a sliding scale. Identifying where your team sits now is the first step toward intentional transformation.

All-Remote
No office, no preferred time zone. Async communication, heavy documentation, maximum flexibility. GitLab's model.
Remote-First
Optimised for remote with documentation and workflows that assume everyone is distributed — even if some visit an office.
Remote-Only
No co-located office, but still biased towards one time zone. Some companies maintain "core team hours."
Remote Allowed
Anyone can work remotely some of the time, with few exceptions for location-dependent roles.
Hybrid-Remote
Some employees work remotely 100% of the time; others are onsite. Tempting compromise, but full of pitfalls (see below).
Remote Days
Entire company — executives included — works remotely on the same designated days.
Remote Exceptions
A few employees can work remotely indefinitely; most are required to be in-office.
Remote Time
Also "remote tolerated" — employees may work some days outside the office, typically offered as a perk.
No Remote
Remote not allowed, due to leadership mandate or the nature of the business.
02 — The hybrid trap

Hybrid-Remote Isn't a Bandaid

Warning: Many leaders underestimate the chaos of a two-tier work environment. Hybrid can seem like the best of both worlds — in practice, the employee experience varies dramatically when remote practices aren't truly embedded in everything you do.

⚠️ Pitfalls of Hybrid

🛰️
Feeling of being a satellite office
📵
Less access to information
😟
Managing guilt with coworkers
❓
Lack of consistent support for remote work
⭐
Demands for overperformance
🏢
Culture built around in-office experience
🎁
Lack of access to in-office perks and benefits

✅ How to Fix Hybrid

Reconsider Required Office Days
Mandating in-office days inhibits relocation, prevents borderless hiring, and stifles remote-first workflows.
Leaders: Stay Home Too
Leaders being remote provides equitable "face time," forces remote-first tool use, and signals the office isn't a power centre.
Redesign Meeting Spaces
Optimise for individual meeting pods so collaboration is equally accessible. Treat the office as just another remote venue.
Coffee Chats for Everyone
Don't allow exclusive coffee chat pairing based on who's onsite — this signals a two-tier work environment.
Record Conversations
Ad hoc hallway chats can seem harmless. Leaders should document or record work conversations to increase transparency.
Equitable Perks
Have an onsite gym? Provide a fitness credit for remote workers. Match all spoken and unspoken benefits.
Share Agendas Up Front
An upfront agenda for every meeting ensures business continues even if 100% of staff choose to work remotely on any given day.
03 — Getting started

The Recipe for a Smooth Transition

Key insight: Remote forces you to do things you should already be doing — but earlier and with greater intention. The three bedrocks of distributed work are documentation, informal communication, and working asynchronously.
Hire a Head of Remote. The ability to thrive remotely is too important to leave to chance. GitLab pioneered this role. Look for someone with extensive remote experience and a history of culture transformation. Can't hire full-time yet? Work with a specialist consulting firm.
1
Make the Executive Team Remote First
Starting at the top sends the clearest signal. It also helps leadership genuinely understand the remote experience so remote-first practices are taken seriously.
2
Establish a Remote Infrastructure
Ask: could your company function if every team member worked from home tomorrow? Identify the gaps in tools and communication now.
3
Document the Culture
Start a living company handbook. Consider every unwritten or implied cultural norm and write it down. In remote settings, there are no daily cues — you must over-communicate values.
4
Equip, Educate, and Empower the Team
Remote work can be jarring for those whose only experience was pandemic-era. Clear proactive communication removes fear and builds excitement about autonomy.
5
Embrace Iteration and Transparency
Expect bumps. Communicate this proactively. Implement the smallest viable change, solicit feedback, and tweak or revert as needed.
6
Structure as if Everyone is Remote
If you maintain any office space, be vigilant that hybrid-remote downsides are not tolerated. Structure processes as if 100% distributed.
7
Enable Healthy Workspaces
Provide each team member a budget to choose their own equipment. Understand there's no one-size-fits-all. Encourage experimenting with home, coworking spaces, and more.

❌ What NOT to Do

✕
Assume there are no resources available yet
✕
Replicate the in-office experience, remotely
✕
Transfer all in-person meetings to virtual
✕
Assume everyone has access to an optimal workspace
✕
Assume remote transformation happens overnight
✕
Assume remote management is drastically different
✕
Assume your existing values can remain static
04 — Leadership

Managing a Remote Team

Core principle: Don't track your team — track their results. Monitoring working hours or laptop activity does nothing but break trust and hinder productivity. Focus on output, not hours spent.

05 — Growing the team

Hiring & Onboarding

Hiring Tips

1
Communicate transparently
Don't make the hiring process a mystery. Set expectations early so candidates can focus on fit.
2
Hire for "values fit"
Not "culture fit." Look for candidates who align with your documented values — it's more inclusive and consistent.
3
Strong remote employee qualities
Timeliness, dependability, collaboration, empathy, kindness, autonomy, self-learning, and communication skills.
4
Use video calls
Inform candidates ahead of time. Give them time to prepare and ensure a stable connection.

Onboarding Best Practices

1
Outline logistics & expectations
Document processes, how-tos, and policies centrally so new hires can self-serve from day one.
2
Identify concerns & answer questions
Actively invite new members to ask questions, especially in the first weeks.
3
Add some excitement
Invest in socialisation so new team members feel genuinely connected — not just professionally, but as humans.
4
Schedule regular check-ins
Weekly 1-1s for work + coffee chats purely for connection. Both matter.
Efficient onboarding delivers:
Higher retention Faster productivity Lower anxiety Clear expectations
06 — Working async

Communication Strategies

The prerequisite to async is documentation. Async communication IS documentation. It's delivering a message in a way that doesn't require the recipient to be available — or even awake — at the same time. Establish standardised documentation first, then build async on top.
🔄 Async Work Principles
  • Async communication = eliminating unnecessary meetings
  • You're not expected to respond immediately — email on weekend? Reply Monday
  • Make meetings optional by recording everything and sharing agendas
  • Try "async weeks" — whole weeks dedicated to declining meetings
  • Each meeting needs an agenda + Google Doc attached to the invite
  • Meeting organiser is responsible for documenting outcomes (acts as a natural filter)
  • Managers: model the behaviour — manage your own calendar the same way
💬 Formalise Informal Communication
  • Remote teams have no hallways or carpools — leaders must actively engineer informal connection
  • People with genuine friends at work perform better and stay longer
  • Design an atmosphere where all team members get to know each other as humans
  • Cancel Zoom happy hours — they cause fatigue and aren't inclusive
  • Try community impact outings: redirect the hour to local community activities
  • Be creative: show-and-tell, virtual talent shows, scavenger hunts

07 — What holds it together

Building Culture

Critical shift: Remote culture can't be shaped by office décor or free snacks. Your culture is the values you write down AND what your leadership team does every day to reinforce those values. There should be no unwritten rules.

Values Must Be Lived

  • →Values only shape an organisation if respected and lived by every team member
  • →With less physical interaction, there's less to compensate for value-violating behaviour
  • →Values infuse everything — including how you promote and recognise people
  • →Respected values serve as a universal north star for distributed teams

    08 — People first

    Mental Health & Time Away

    This is a team sport. Leaders must build the culture. Managers must sense signs of mental strain proactively. Team members must feel safe surfacing issues while they're still manageable.
    📖
    Document Mental Health Processes
    Isolation is amplified remotely. Ensure mental health resources are in the central company handbook. Highlight them during onboarding and reinforce in L&D sessions.
    🌿
    Encourage a Healthy Lifestyle
    Remote enables structuring work around life, not vice versa. Encourage routines — or non-linear workdays. Support peak-productivity scheduling. Don't track your team.
    🤝
    Create a Non-Judgmental Culture
    Destigmatise mental health conversations. Celebrate diversity and create an environment where team members can be their authentic selves at work.
    ⏱️
    Don't Celebrate Long Hours
    Burnout creeps up over days and weeks. Be careful when publicly praising "going above and beyond" — don't signal that work should always take priority over life.
    🌙
    Rest is Productive
    Clarity comes from time away from work. Rest isn't at the expense of work — it's a core function of doing excellent work. Balance work ethic with rest ethic.
    📅
    Be Proactive About PTO
    Block "potential" PTO on your calendar in advance to remind yourself to take time away — even if it's simply a day to spend outdoors. Encourage your team to do the same.
    09 — Growing bigger

    Remote at Scale

    Counterintuitive truth: Remote work doesn't just work at scale — it works better at scale than a colocated model. Fully distributed organisations with no offices to maintain unlock outsized agility and efficiency as they grow.
    Team Growth
    All-remote companies grow faster. No office expansions, no relocation requests, no real estate budget. Leadership focuses purely on finding and onboarding the right talent.
    Better Hiring
    Opening your pipeline globally means reaching a more diverse, talented applicant pool that most companies never access. Skills and results replace location as the filter.
    Reduced Ambiguity
    Remote teams document culture and processes from the start. Everyone — regardless of time zone — has access to the same critical information, reducing confusion at scale.
    Knowledge Dissemination
    In colocated teams, new hire expertise only benefits those nearby. All-remote means every new hire's knowledge benefits the entire organisation equally — creating a more transparent environment.
