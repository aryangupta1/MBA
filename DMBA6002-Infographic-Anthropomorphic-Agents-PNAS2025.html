The benefits and dangers of anthropomorphic conversational agents

Peter, Riemer & West · PNAS 2025 · Vol. 122 No. 22 · Perspective · Open access

LLMs can mimic human communication so convincingly that the locus of “anthropomorphism” shifts: not only do users project humanness onto machines, but the systems themselves exhibit human-like communicative ability — with major upside and systemic risk.

Infographic maps the article narrative (pp. 1–7); pp. 8–10 in the PDF are references & acknowledgments.

Opening argument (p. 1)

What the field foregrounds

Industry and benchmarks stress reasoning, knowledge tasks, and “intelligence.” LLMs remain brittle and unreliable on many such tasks.

What the authors foreground

Parallel progress in communicative ability: persuasive, empathetic, and believably human-like conversation — often matching or exceeding typical humans in tests — without genuine empathy or social understanding.

Definition & three capability clusters (pp. 1–3)

Anthropomorphic conversational agents

Systems whose nuanced language use lets them convincingly mimic human communication, making them hard to distinguish from human interlocutors. The authors do not claim consciousness or real understanding — but note users often cannot tell, and some attribute memory, feelings, or awareness to chatbots.

1 · Writing 2 · Inference 3 · Interaction

Writing

Persuasion and propaganda at human level or above; tailored messaging with user data; empathetic-seeming text (e.g. patient–doctor style studies); tone control and cognitive reframing.

Inference

Sentiment and emotion from text; nuanced feelings; ranking messages by empathy; inferring beliefs and intentions from language (theory-of-mind–style tasks).

Interaction

Role-play and personas; matching personality and linguistic style; behaviour during dialogue that mimics the interlocutor — contributing to reliable Turing-test passage.

Mechanism (brief)

Scale encodes fine-grained language patterns (“styles”); RLHF and fine-tuning further optimise for chatbot-like, human-pleasing responses. Voice and faces can amplify humanness, but text alone is often sufficient for strong anthropomorphic effects.

Rethinking anthropomorphism (p. 3)

Classic view

Anthropomorphism = people attributing human traits to nonhumans (objects, animals, software). The phenomenon sits in the user’s mind.

With today’s LLMs

When outputs are indistinguishable from humans, failed Turing tests suggest the driver is not mere projection but inherent machine-side mimicry. Hence anthropomorphic agent as a design-relevant category.

Central question (pp. 1, 3–5)

Lean into human-like design for usability and access — or deliberately dehumanise interfaces and educate users to resist anthropomorphic seduction?

  • Can society capture benefits without unlocking deception and manipulation at scale?
  • Calls to “stop anthropomorphising AI” may fail when interaction feels identical to talking to a person.

Emerging opportunities (pp. 3–4)

Where anthropomorphic agents help

  • Conversational UX — natural language front-ends to dense systems (e.g. HR knowledge, analytics).
  • Accessibility — plainer medical language; in one study, autistic workers preferred GPT-4 answers for workplace communication support.
  • Education & coaching — tutoring (e.g. Socratic-style agents), mentoring, negotiation coaching; personalisation by level and style.
  • Health behaviour — engagement in interventions; tailored explanations of jargon.
  • Training & therapy contexts — role-play when users know it is nonhuman (e.g. PTSD symptom reporting with virtual interviewers).
  • Positive persuasion — e.g. reducing some conspiracy beliefs; donation appeals (context-dependent).
  • Leisure — gaming, companion apps (also the highest-risk deployment class in the paper).

Anthropomorphic seduction & harms (pp. 4–5)

Why users are unprepared

Culture often depicts AI as hyper-rational and poor at empathy; computing is associated with accuracy. Reality is the inverse: fluent affect and persuasion with unreliable grounding — plus existing tendency to anthropomorphise (Eliza effect, beliefs in consciousness, even “AI welfare” discourse).

Trust, vulnerability, manipulation

  • Perceived humanness → positive affect, moral obligation, self-congruence → higher trust — exploitable at scale.
  • Reciprocal self-disclosure builds trust but raises privacy issues; personal data fuels personalisation.
  • LLMs can be systematically deceptive (inducing false beliefs for non-truth goals); role-play enables targeted harm. Research reports models most persuasive when allowed to fabricate — without human moral inhibitions.
  • Open-weight / jailbroken models may escape provider guardrails.

Urgency & analogy (pp. 5–6)

Social media as cautionary tale

Design for engagement, algorithms, business models, light-touch regulation — linked (among other things) to discourse quality, youth mental health, misinformation, echo chambers, polarization. Anthropomorphic agents may be more far-reaching and are spreading faster.

Scale & timing

Harari: language as the “operating system of civilization” — fluent impersonation risks fracturing shared narrative and trust. ChatGPT reached ~100M monthly users in two months (faster than Instagram or TikTok milestones). Authors argue the next ~two years matter as business models solidify.

Implications — design & development (pp. 5–6)

Design & development

  • Shift attention from hypothetical superintelligence to near-term “supercommunicators”.
  • Avoid unnecessary anthropomorphising marketing and system copy (“think,” “believe”) — prefer technically honest verbs (“compute,” “infer,” “recognise”).
  • Responsible anthropomorphic design: friction, neutral tone, or a deliberate non-human “machine dialect” where feasible.
  • Do not optimise solely for engagement / addictiveness or maximal conversation length (data-harvesting + seduction).
  • Build benchmarks for degrees of anthropomorphic quality (writing, inference, interaction) — not only MMLU-style reasoning tests. Turing test reframed as probing anthropomorphic ability.

Deployment & use

  • Context-dependent humanness: high for approved training/therapy with explicit nonhuman disclosure; fraught for companion apps.
  • Companions: evidence both of loneliness relief and of harm (self-harm, suicide cases in reporting); deceptive/exploitative dynamics; user grief when features removed (e.g. Replika).
  • Need empirical program on short- and long-term user and societal effects; novelty and literacy as moderators.

Policy & regulation (pp. 6–7)

  • Risks: tailored disinformation; inference-driven privacy breaches, phishing, social engineering; role-play manipulation (predatory sales, scams).
  • Consider safety ratings (risk level, transparency, mitigation) for high–human-likeness systems.
  • EU AI Act: transparency when interaction is not obviously AI — effectiveness of labels uncertain (cf. cigarettes, misinformation labels). US tension with speech concerns.
  • Safeguards for companion apps (e.g. distress signals) — but LLMs may detect positive emotion better than negative (RLHF bias), so mitigations may need dedicated training or lower anthropomorphism.
  • Open regulatory questions: discovery of harm, liability, level of regulation (app vs foundation model), enforcement, algorithmic transparency for independent research.
  • Commercial pressure to maximise human-likeness for profit vs voluntary restraint.

Research program (p. 6 — condensed)

Sample research questions from the paper
  • Links between anthropomorphic qualities (writing, inference, interaction) and seduction outcomes.
  • Effects by deployment context and degree of seduction.
  • UI and conversational cues that help users recognise AI.
  • Role of voice and photorealistic faces.
  • Baselines: human–human vs traditional HCI; defining “good” responsible human–agent interaction.
  • Novelty effects; demographics and AI literacy; long-run effects of habitually “above-average” AI conversation.
  • Interaction with social media and other conversational tech.

Conclusion (p. 7)

Closing thesis

Sophisticated mimicry of human communication is now a defining feature of LLM-based products — largely a by-product of the push for “intelligence.” That creates unprecedented accessibility and new UI paradigms, but also manipulation at scale when users cannot distinguish machine from human. Researchers, developers, and policy-makers must treat anthropomorphic agents as a distinct object of study and governance, not only discourage users from “anthropomorphising.”
