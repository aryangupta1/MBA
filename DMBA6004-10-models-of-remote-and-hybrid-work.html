The 10 Models of Remote & Hybrid Work

A definitive framework for understanding every stage on the remote-work spectrum — from "no remote" to fully "all-remote" — and what each one means in practice.

The Spectrum at a Glance
From Fully In-Office → Fully Distributed
1 · No Remote 5 · Remote Days 10 · All-Remote ✦

GitLab defines 10 distinct models organisations use for remote and hybrid work. These aren't arbitrary — each represents a meaningfully different set of policies, cultural defaults, and operational norms. Understanding where you sit on this spectrum is the prerequisite to intentional improvement. The models are ordered from least to most remote-friendly, though some organisations blend elements across stages.

1 🏢
No Remote
Fully In-Office

Remote work is not allowed. All employees must work from a company office. This is typically driven by a leadership mandate, the nature of the business (e.g. manufacturing, retail), or a belief that in-person work is required for culture or productivity. There is no policy framework for remote work at all.

No async culture Talent pool limited to commute radius High real-estate cost Least agile to crises
2 🚪
Remote Exceptions
Rare Allowances

A small number of individuals are allowed to work remotely on a permanent basis — typically due to unique circumstances, relocation, or a key hire negotiation — while the vast majority are required to work in-office. There is no systematic or equitable framework; exceptions are ad-hoc and can create resentment among those not granted them.

Inconsistent & inequitable No remote infrastructure Can create two-tier culture Minimal flexibility
3 📅
Remote Time / Remote Tolerated
Perk-Level Remote

Employees are permitted to work some days from outside the office, but this is positioned as a perk rather than a strategic policy. There is no expectation that remote days will be treated the same as in-office days — meetings are still held in-person and remote workers may feel excluded. Often used as a recruiting or retention sweetener with no real operational support behind it.

Remote treated as privilege, not policy No documentation culture Some scheduling flexibility FOMO for remote workers
4 🗓️
Remote Days
Scheduled Flexibility

The entire company — including executives — works remotely on the same designated days each week. This is a meaningful step: leadership participates equally, which helps reduce the stigma of being remote. However, because it applies uniformly to a schedule rather than individual need, it still lacks the flexibility and infrastructure of a truly remote-first model.

Executives participate equally Normalises remote Still tied to fixed schedule No async infrastructure required
5 🏠
Remote Allowed
Opt-In Remote

Any employee can work remotely some or all of the time, with very few exceptions for genuinely location-dependent roles (e.g. front-desk, lab work). There is no requirement to go into an office, but the company hasn't fully optimised for remote either — office-centric habits and culture may still dominate. It's a meaningful policy step, but without remote-first infrastructure, remote workers are still second-class.

Broad eligibility Employee autonomy increases Office may still be cultural default Remote workers may feel marginalised
6 🔀
Hybrid-Remote
Mixed Model

Some employees work remotely 100% of the time; others work onsite in at least one physical office. This is the most common post-pandemic model, but GitLab cautions it requires enormous intentionality to execute well. Without deliberate effort, it defaults into a two-tier system where in-office workers enjoy greater access, visibility, and advancement opportunities. There are four hybrid sub-types: office-centric, flexible, remote-friendly, and virtual-first — each with meaningfully different outcomes.

Flexibility for diverse needs Risk of two-tier work environment Requires extreme intentionality Harder to build unified culture Most common post-pandemic model
7 🌐
Remote-Friendly
Thoughtful Hybrid

A more mature hybrid form where the organisation actively works to ensure remote employees aren't disadvantaged. Meetings are designed to be accessible remotely, information is documented, and leaders model remote-inclusive behaviour. However, there is still a physical office, and the company hasn't fully committed to treating every location equally — the office retains some social and cultural gravity.

Active inclusion of remote workers Documented processes Office still exists & has cultural pull Equity requires constant maintenance
8 📖
Remote-First
Distributed by Design

The company is structurally optimised for remote work. Documentation, policies, workflows, and tools all assume that everyone is distributed — even if some team members occasionally visit a shared office space. Meetings are designed for remote participants first. The office, if it exists, is treated simply as another optional co-working venue, not a seat of power or cultural centre. A major step toward fully distributed operations.

Documentation-first culture Office is optional co-working space Async workflows standard Equal experience for all locations Optional physical presence
9 ⏰
Remote-Only
No Office, Some Sync

There is no shared physical office — all work is conducted remotely. However, the company still operates with a strong synchronous bias, typically aligned to one time zone or set of "core hours." Team members are generally expected to be online and available at the same time. This model captures the real-estate and talent-pool benefits of remote, but doesn't yet unlock the full productivity and autonomy advantages of async-first work.

No office to maintain Broader talent hiring pool Still biased toward one time zone Core hours limit flexibility No true async culture
10 ✦
All-Remote
GitLab's Model · The Gold Standard

The most advanced and intentional model. No office exists, there is no preferred time zone, and work is structured around asynchronous communication and comprehensive documentation. A strong bias toward async encourages writing things down over calling a meeting, enables work across every time zone, and gives individuals maximum autonomy over their schedule. GitLab has operated this way since inception with 2,000+ team members in 65+ countries. Requires the most cultural investment, but delivers the greatest benefits in diversity, talent access, autonomy, and organisational resilience.

No office, no real-estate overhead Hire from anywhere in the world Full async-first culture No time zone preferences Maximum individual autonomy Documentation is institutional memory Requires significant cultural investment GitLab's model since inception
Deep Dive

The 4 Hybrid-Remote Sub-Models

Stage 6 (Hybrid-Remote) contains four meaningfully different approaches. Understanding which sub-model you're running determines the employee experience — and what needs to change.

🏢 Office-Centric Hybrid
The office is the default. Remote work is permitted but it's the exception, not the expectation. Culture, decisions, and career advancement all happen primarily in-person. Remote workers are at a structural disadvantage. This is the riskiest sub-model for talent retention.
📋 Flexible Hybrid
Employees choose where they work each day with no fixed requirements. Some teams or roles may have more structure than others. Works well in principle but can create unpredictability and unequal experiences across teams without a clear remote-first framework underpinning it.
🌐 Remote-Friendly Hybrid
Deliberate investment in making remote work equitable. Meetings are designed for remote-first participation. Leaders actively model remote-inclusive behaviour. Documentation is prioritised. Still has an office, but it's no longer the cultural default — a healthy middle ground if maintained with discipline.
💻 Virtual-First
The closest hybrid gets to all-remote. The default interaction is digital, and the office (if it exists) is used primarily for intentional in-person gatherings — team summits, onboarding cohorts, or brainstorming sessions. Async workflows dominate. Closest to remote-first on the spectrum without fully eliminating the physical space.

Most organisations skip stages rather than moving linearly. The most common leap is from Stage 6 (Hybrid) to Stage 8 (Remote-First) — a shift that requires documentation culture, async workflows, leadership buy-in, and often a dedicated Head of Remote. No stage is inherently bad — what matters is that your model is intentional and your team understands it.

Key insight from GitLab: The terminology you use matters. Many organisations call themselves "hybrid" but are actually running an office-centric model with remote exceptions. Honestly naming which model you operate helps leadership understand the gap between aspiration and reality — and what investment is needed to close it. The first step is always honest self-assessment.
What All Advanced Stages Have in Common

The 3 Pillars of Remote Maturity

📝 Documentation Culture
Every model from Stage 8 onward requires a deeply embedded documentation-first mindset. Information must be written down in a shared, searchable, living handbook — not locked in someone's head or a chat thread. This is the single most reliable differentiator between functional and dysfunctional remote models. Without it, async doesn't work.
🔄 Async-First Workflows
Defaulting to asynchronous communication — meaning you don't need to be online at the same time to collaborate — is the unlock for global teams and flexible schedules. This requires discipline: recording meetings, attaching agendas, writing outcomes in docs, and giving team members time to respond on their own schedule rather than immediately.
🤝 Intentional Informal Connection
Remote companies don't have hallways or water coolers. Leaders must deliberately engineer informal connection: coffee chats, social calls, shared interest channels, AMAs, and periodic in-person gatherings. Culture doesn't maintain itself remotely — it requires active, structured investment. This becomes more critical the higher your stage number.
