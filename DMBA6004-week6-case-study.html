Question 01 · Erin Meyer
Using Erin Meyer’s framework, how would you classify Giant Swarm’s dominant leadership and decision-making culture? Is it Egalitarian/Consensual, Egalitarian/Top-down, or something else? Justify your answer with evidence from the case.
① Answer
② Script
③ Slide
Answer

Dominantly egalitarian & consensual (with pragmatic execution)

Classification

Primarily Egalitarian / Consensual — minimal hierarchy, self-organisation, and an explicit expectation to speak up and contribute.

  • Flat-ish authority: not heavily concentrated in formal managers.
  • Speak-up culture: employees are expected to raise issues and contribute openly.
  • Consultative processes: teams, chapters, SIGs and RFC-style async discussion support consensus-building.
  • Nuance: not “endless consensus” — speed and initiative matter, so execution can look more top-down once enough input is gathered.

Bottom line: Giant Swarm’s leadership culture is best described as egalitarian/consensual, with pragmatic flexibility to move quickly when needed.

Script
Giant Swarm’s dominant leadership and decision-making culture is best classified as primarily egalitarian and consensual. The case shows that the company has minimal hierarchy, values self-organisation, and expects employees to speak up, take responsibility, and act like trusted adults. That strongly aligns with an egalitarian culture because authority is not based heavily on rank or formal management layers. It also leans strongly toward consensual decision-making because teams are expected to contribute input, discussions happen openly, and knowledge-sharing structures like teams, chapters, SIGs, and RFC-style processes all support consultation before action. So decisions are shaped collectively rather than simply handed down. That said, I would not call Giant Swarm purely egalitarian-consensual in an absolute sense. Because it is a remote-first tech company operating in a complex and fast-moving environment, it also needs speed and initiative. So in practice, some decisions may still happen more quickly once enough input has been gathered. Overall, though, the dominant culture is clearly egalitarian and consensual, with some pragmatic flexibility in execution.

Question 02 · Cross-cultural onboarding
Imagine Giant Swarm hires two new senior engineers: one from Japan (typically Hierarchical/Consensual) and one from the USA (typically Egalitarian/Top-down). What specific misunderstandings or challenges might arise for each of them within Giant Swarm’s existing culture? How could Giant Swarm’s onboarding process be adapted to address these potential cultural frictions?
① Answer
② Script
③ Slide
Answer

Different assumptions about authority, voice, and decision speed

Japanese engineer (Hierarchical / Consensual)
  • May expect clearer authority and guidance before acting.
  • May hesitate to challenge ideas publicly or act without direction.
  • Could be misread as passive or low-confidence when it’s cultural caution.
US engineer (Egalitarian / Top-down)
  • May expect quicker decisions and clearer decision ownership after discussion.
  • May see alignment-seeking as slow or vague.
  • Directness could be perceived as impatience or dominance.

Onboarding adaptations

  • Make decision norms explicit: how decisions are made, who owns what, how disagreement works.
  • Show concrete examples of “speaking up” and “autonomy” in practice.
  • Buddy support for cultural navigation, not just tools/admin.
  • Clarify the paradox: autonomy ≠ isolation; consultation ≠ indecision.
Script
For the Japanese engineer, the biggest challenge would likely be Giant Swarm’s expectation of visible autonomy and open participation. Someone from a more hierarchical and consensual background may expect clearer authority, more guidance, and more careful alignment before speaking up or acting. In Giant Swarm, that could create hesitation, and their behaviour might be misread as low initiative when it is actually cultural caution. For the US engineer, the likely challenge is the opposite. They may be comfortable speaking up and operating in a flat structure, but they may expect faster decision-making and clearer ownership once discussion begins. Giant Swarm’s collaborative and alignment-oriented style could feel slower or less defined than expected, and they may push for action before others feel ready. So the onboarding process should be adapted to make these norms explicit. Giant Swarm should clearly explain how decisions are made, what kind of participation is expected, how autonomy works, and how disagreement is handled. A buddy system should also help translate the culture in practice, not just help with logistics. The main point is that cultural friction here comes less from capability and more from different assumptions about authority, communication, and decision-making.

Question 03 · Inclusion design
Giant Swarm’s culture is described as having “minimal hierarchy” and valuing “speaking up.” How might this approach need to be intentionally managed or communicated differently to be effective and inclusive for employees from cultures with high Power Distance (as per Hofstede) or a more hierarchical background?
① Answer
② Script
③ Slide
Answer

Flat structure ≠ equal participation (without intentional design)

  • In high power-distance cultures, “speaking up” may feel disrespectful or risky.
  • Silence can be misread as disengagement; confident voices can dominate.
  • Removing formal hierarchy doesn’t automatically create psychological safety.
What to do differently
  • Make norms explicit: participation is valued; respectful disagreement is acceptable.
  • Define “how to disagree”: constructive challenge, not confrontation.
  • Design for inclusion: structured turn-taking, active invitations to quieter voices.
  • Use async channels: written input lowers the social cost of challenging authority.
  • Check understanding: leaders actively verify alignment rather than assuming it.

Key insight: inclusion in a flat culture requires intentional support — otherwise openness benefits the people already culturally comfortable being open.

Script
Giant Swarm’s minimal hierarchy and speak-up culture sound very inclusive on the surface, but they do not automatically work for everyone in the same way. For employees from high power distance or more hierarchical backgrounds, speaking up may feel uncomfortable because respect is often shown through deference, not open challenge. So if Giant Swarm simply assumes everyone will contribute in the same way, silence may be misread as disengagement rather than a cultural difference. To make this culture more effective and inclusive, Giant Swarm needs to communicate its norms much more explicitly. It should clearly explain that respectful disagreement is valued, what good participation looks like, and how people are expected to contribute. Leaders also need to actively create space for input rather than assuming an open floor is enough. That could include inviting quieter people directly, using async channels, and structuring discussions so contribution does not depend only on confidence. The key point is that flat culture is not automatically inclusive. It has to be intentionally supported if Giant Swarm wants employees from different cultural backgrounds to participate fully.
