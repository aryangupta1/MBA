Meet the workplace vibers — powered by AI

University of Sydney News & Opinion · 13 Mar 2025 · Originally Australian Financial Review

A visual summary of Riemer & Peter on performing expertise versus building it — and what leaders can do about it.

Story · “Phil”

A marketing manager becomes the team’s “content wizard”: slick decks and copy, endless ideas — but no original sentences in months. Prompt, pick what “feels right,” light edit, ship. The archetype of performing expertise without earning it.

Who is a viber?

Colleagues who present polished ideas they struggle to defend; confident on the surface until challenged; strategy that collapses into vague platitudes under scrutiny.

Core idea

Vibers can perform understanding without achieving it — using AI to simulate expertise in domains they have not mastered. They can fake it and never need to make it.

Good vibes (skilful use)

  • Amplifies strong ideas and productivity
  • Explores perspectives, structures complexity, refines narratives
  • Brainstorms, experiments — extends your thinking

Bad vibes (autopilot)

  • Prioritises output that “feels right” over critical thinking
  • A subtle shift toward “Looks good to me” (LGTM) — approving AI output without real scrutiny
  • Mistakes task completion for proficiency (e.g. essays submitted with minimal editing)
  • Mistakes avoiding hard thinking for intuitive leadership (e.g. accepting AI recommendations uncritically)
  • Even skilled workers drift toward uncritical AI reliance over time

“Vibe coding” (Karpathy)

Coined for throwaway weekend builds — amusing there, risky when scaled to work that must hold up.

From the original piece

“I ‘Accept All’ always, I don’t read the diffs anymore… The code grows beyond my usual comprehension… It’s not too bad for throwaway weekend projects… I just see stuff, say stuff, run stuff, and copy paste stuff, and it mostly works.” — Andrej Karpathy (via article)

Why it matters for organisations

Unchecked vibing makes organisations brittle: homogenisation toward the average, errors that surface late, and customer-facing failure down the line. Models marketed for reasoning (e.g. OpenAI’s o3, Anthropic’s Claude 3.7) produce compelling outputs — still require checks for accuracy and reliability.

Speed rewarded over depth
Information overload
Remote, async collaboration

Upskilling response

  • Tech fluency — how models work, intentional use, risks of outsourcing reasoning
  • Combat vibification through understanding, not prohibition alone

Leadership response

  • AI literacy for leaders
  • Shape culture: expertise, responsible tech use, and recrafting org design with AI in mind

Takeaway

AI is a fixture with real upside. Understand the tool and trust your own thinking — then you get productive assistance without the bad vibes.
