Reading Overview

Technology strategy is an integrated set of choices about how to use new technology to create competitive advantage, manage uncertainty, choose where to compete, and capture value as industries evolve.

Big Picture

Technology changes the playing field

New technology can create entirely new offerings, redraw industry boundaries, and undermine existing advantages.

Strategy must be dynamic

Unlike conventional strategy, technology strategy operates under uncertainty, changing ecosystems, and shifting market structure.

Value capture is never automatic

Creating innovation is not enough. Firms still need commercialization logic, bargaining power, and control over scarce resources.

What’s Different About Technology Strategy?

Conventional strategy assumes known markets and proven technologies. Technology strategy deals with new ways of producing value, uncertain customers, changing ecosystems, and the possibility of creative destruction.

Core Distinction

1. New technology creates new choices

Firms are not just choosing a position within an industry. They may be choosing which industry to enter, what value proposition becomes possible, and what new competitive advantage can be built.

2. Risk and uncertainty are structural

Technology may fail, customer value may be unclear, and key complements may not yet exist. This makes forecasting and staged decision-making critical.

3. Ecosystems matter

Many innovations require suppliers, partners, developers, or distributors to make the offering viable. Strategy therefore includes how to work with or against an ecosystem.

Technology strategy is ultimately about more than adoption. It is about using technological change to redefine how the firm creates, delivers, and captures value over time.

S-Curves: Performance and Adoption

The reading emphasizes two linked curves: the technology S-curve and the market diffusion S-curve. Strategy depends on where the technology is maturing and how fast the market is adopting.

Timing Logic

Technology S-Curve

Performance improves slowly at first, then rapidly, then plateaus as limits are reached.

Market Diffusion S-Curve

Adoption begins slowly, accelerates once legitimacy increases, then slows as the market approaches saturation.

Why this matters strategically

  • Invest too early and the technology or market may not be ready.
  • Wait too long and another firm may shape the standard or dominate the installed base.
  • Technology and market timing rarely move at exactly the same speed.

Three recurring stage questions

  • Introduction: Does it work? Who will adopt it? How do we commercialize it?
  • Growth: How do we grow the market and capture value?
  • Maturity: How do we respond to the next technology transition?

The Innovator Journey

Innovators face a changing strategic agenda as technology and markets evolve. The reading frames this journey through introduction, growth, and maturity.

Lifecycle View
Stage 1

Introduction — proving the idea

Reduce technical risk cheaply, identify the strongest customer need, and decide how the idea should be commercialized. Early prototypes, pilots, and experiments are strategic tools, not just technical exercises.

Stage 2

Growth — crossing into the mainstream

Winning now depends on product-market fit for the early majority, niche domination, improvement of the value proposition, bargaining power, ecosystem positioning, and often aggressive moves to build scale.

Stage 3

Maturity — adapting before decline

Once growth slows, firms must either extend the offering into adjacent markets or transition to the next technological wave. The danger is optimizing yesterday’s model while missing tomorrow’s discontinuity.

Commercialization Strategy

A new technology is an idea. The commercialization problem is deciding how to make money from that idea. The reading frames this as a choice between cooperation and competition depending on two things: the strength of the market for ideas and ownership of complementary assets.

Cooperate or Compete
No efficient market for ideas + no incumbent assets

Attacker’s Advantage

Enter the product market and attack incumbents before they imitate. Useful when the incumbent cannot add much value and cooperation is unattractive.

No efficient market for ideas + incumbent has assets

Reputation-Based Ideas Trading

Cooperation can still happen if the incumbent is known for paying fairly rather than appropriating ideas opportunistically.

Efficient market for ideas + no incumbent assets

Greenfield Competition

Race among innovators in a new space. Advantage often comes from speed, adoption, and setting the rules of the emerging market.

Efficient market for ideas + incumbent has assets

Ideas Factories

License or sell to firms with the complementary assets needed to scale. Common when IP is stronger and incumbents can commercialize efficiently.

Why complementary assets matter
Manufacturing, distribution, brand credibility, service capability, installed base, and regulatory infrastructure can determine whether a technology reaches customers at all. Owning the innovation does not guarantee capturing the value if another player controls the scarce complements.
The paradox of disclosure
To sell an idea, the innovator often has to reveal enough information for the buyer to assess it. But the more the innovator reveals, the easier imitation may become. This weakens bargaining power unless IP protection or reputation is strong.
Revenue models in emerging markets
Firms may experiment with subscriptions, freemium, advertising, complement pricing, or platform commissions. In new markets, the customers who generate revenue may be different from the users who initially adopt the product.

Growth, Adoption, and Competitive Advantage

Once commercialized, the challenge becomes growing the market while still capturing a meaningful share of the value created.

Execution Stage

Crossing the chasm

  • Early adopters tolerate novelty and imperfection.
  • Mainstream customers want reliability, support, compatibility, and reduced risk.
  • The most reliable path is to dominate a tightly defined niche first, then expand.

Capturing value during growth

  • Improve the value proposition continuously.
  • Preempt competitors through speed, acquisitions, or early commitments.
  • Protect and leverage IP.
  • Exploit switching costs, scale, and network effects where possible.

To Lead or to Follow?

Being first to market is not the same as being first to scale or first to mass adoption. The reading argues that timing should be judged by the type of market, pace of technological change, and the firm’s resources.

Timing Choice

When leading can pay off

  • You can lock in customers before switching costs rise.
  • You can preempt scarce assets, standards, or ecosystem relationships.
  • You can achieve scale or network effects before rivals.
  • You can sustain a technical learning advantage.

When following can be smarter

  • Technology is changing quickly and first versions will likely be leapfrogged.
  • The market is still fragmented and no dominant design exists yet.
  • You have complementary assets that let you enter later and consolidate the market.
  • You can learn from pioneers without bearing all the early risk.
Real advantage comes not from being early by itself, but from converting timing into customer lock-in, scale, standards, ecosystem power, or durable differentiation.

Strategies for New Markets vs Existing Markets

The logic of technology strategy changes depending on whether the technology creates an entirely new market or attacks an existing one.

Context Matters

New markets

  • There may be no incumbent to displace, but also no proven demand.
  • Early markets often have incompatible designs and fragmented offerings.
  • Fast second movers with resources may standardize the market and capture most value.

Existing markets

  • Entrants must take share from incumbents.
  • Incumbents need road maps, switching-cost defenses, and awareness of disruptive technologies.
  • Attackers need a significant technological gap plus ecosystem support and adoption momentum.
Disruptive technologies and the innovator’s dilemma
Disruptive technologies often look weaker at first on the dimensions current customers care about. Incumbents ignore them because they appear inferior or low margin. But entrants can improve the technology, target overlooked segments, and eventually change the basis of competition entirely.
How incumbents can defend
Forecast transitions with road maps, invest in complementary assets, maintain backward compatibility when helpful, raise switching costs, shape customer expectations, and consider separate units to explore disruptive technologies without suffocating them.

Platform Technologies and Ecosystem Power

Some technologies become platforms because they coordinate suppliers, complementors, and users. Platform leaders often shape industry evolution by controlling key interfaces and benefiting from indirect network effects.

Supplemental Reading

Coring

Turn a product into a platform by solving an essential system problem, making complement creation easier, and keeping enough control to preserve value capture.

Tipping

Build momentum so the market tips toward wide adoption through subsidies, coalition building, complementor incentives, and hard-to-imitate features.

Openness trade-off

Too closed and complements may not develop. Too open and the platform owner may lose the ability to capture value. Platform strategy is therefore a controlled openness problem.

Why platforms are powerful

  • They coordinate multiple sides of a market.
  • They can harness indirect network effects.
  • They may shape standards and partner incentives.
  • They can create ecosystems without surrendering all value capture.

Key strategic questions

  • Does the product solve an essential problem others depend on?
  • How open should the platform be?
  • Will the business act as reseller, marketplace, hybrid, or infrastructure provider?
  • How will it avoid being commoditized by its own ecosystem?

Key Takeaways

Summary

What technology strategy really does

  • It helps firms decide where technological change creates advantage or threat.
  • It links invention, commercialization, market timing, and ecosystem design.
  • It treats uncertainty, complements, and industry evolution as strategic issues rather than background conditions.

What strong strategists must keep asking

  • What value does this technology unlock, and for whom?
  • Do we need to cooperate, compete, or shape the system around us?
  • What scarce resource lets us capture value rather than merely create it?
  • What wave is coming next, and are we positioned to ride it?
