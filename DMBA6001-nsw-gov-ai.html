Core Definition
What is AI?

AI is the ability of a computer system to perform tasks that would normally require human intelligence, such as learning, reasoning, and making decisions.

🧩 Machine Learning 👁️ Computer Vision 💬 Natural Language Processing
Specialised Domains of Artificial Intelligence
✨
Gen AI
Generative AI

An emerging field within AI that creates new content — such as text, images, voice, video, and code — by learning from data patterns.

Notable examples: ChatGPT, Google's Bard

📊
ML
Machine Learning

A subset of AI that allows computers to autonomously learn and improve without being explicitly programmed. ML algorithms are trained on data to make predictions or decisions.

Foundation for most modern AI applications

🗣️
NLP
Natural Language Processing

A field of AI dealing with the ability of computer systems to understand and generate human language. Used to analyse text, comprehend and converse with users, and perform tasks like language translation, sentiment analysis, and question answering.

Powers chatbots, translation tools, voice assistants

👁️
CV
Computer Vision

Empowers computers to 'see' and comprehend the visual world, analysing images and videos like humans. CV algorithms handle object detection, face recognition, and self-driving car navigation.

Object detection · Face recognition · Autonomous vehicles

Terms Commonly Used in AI
Algorithm

A set of instructions that guide a computer in performing specific tasks or solving problems. Algorithms range from simple tasks like sending reminders to complex problem-solving — crucial in AI and ML.

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)

Considered AI's 'holy grail'. AGI represents a level of AI possessing human-like intellect — able to perform any intellectual task a human can do, understanding, learning, and adapting across a wide range of domains, rather than being specialised.

Data Science

A multidisciplinary field extracting insights and knowledge from data using statistical analysis, data mining, machine learning, and data visualisation.

Data Mining

A technique used to analyse large amounts of information to gain insights, spot trends, or uncover patterns. Used for customer segmentation, fraud detection, and market research.

Deep Learning

A machine learning technique using interconnected layers of "neurons" to learn patterns in data — especially for image recognition and speech synthesis. "Deep" refers to many organised layers. Currently the most widely used approach for visual object recognition, machine translation, speech recognition, and image synthesis.

Large Language Model (LLM)

A subset of Gen AI that specialises in generating human-like text. Unlike broader Generative AI (which covers text, images, audio, and video), LLMs focus on language-based generation and understanding.

Multimodal Foundation Model (MfM)

A type of large language model that can process and output multiple data types — including text, images, audio, and video — in a single unified model.

Neural Networks

Computer models inspired by the human brain's structure. Interconnected artificial neurons, organised in layers, learn from data to make predictions — underpinning deep learning.

Reinforcement Learning (RL)

A machine learning approach where algorithms learn by taking actions to achieve a specific goal, guided by rewards or penalties. The AI agent continuously improves decision-making based on these reward signals.

Supervised Learning

A sub-category of machine learning where algorithms learn from labelled data to make predictions or classifications, often with high accuracy. Real-world example: classifying spam emails into a separate folder.

Unsupervised Learning

Named so because it doesn't rely on predefined labelled data. A type of machine learning where algorithms group data objects based on similarities, without prior category specifications.

Transformer

A powerful AI model for understanding and generating human language, widely used in tasks like translation and question answering. The architecture underpinning most modern LLMs.

Trustworthy & Responsible AI

AI systems developed and used ethically, transparently, and accountably — addressing issues such as bias, privacy, and security throughout design and deployment.

Human Oversight Models
HITL
Human-in-the-Loop

A model that requires human interaction. A human plays an active and integral role in decision-making, monitoring, and control. They are part of the loop that produces outcomes, and their input or oversight is crucial to the system's functioning.

🔁
HOTL
Human-on-the-Loop

An extension of HITL. Humans oversee an automated system and provide feedback but are not directly involved in decision-making. The system runs autonomously while humans monitor and intervene when necessary.

Common Applications of AI
💬
Chatbot

Conversational agents that interact with users — such as empathic patient robots or customer service chatbots. Understand human language through Natural Language Processing and assist with various tasks.

📈
Decision Support System

An AI-powered tool that helps organisations analyse data and provide informed decision-making options. Brings together data and knowledge from different areas and sources to go beyond usual reports and summaries.

🏛️
Expert System

An intelligent computer program that emulates the knowledge and expertise of a human expert. Uses information and reasoning to solve difficult, time-intensive problems in fields like medicine, environment, finance, and law.

🎯
Recommendation System

An AI system that suggests personalised content or products to users based on their preferences and behaviour. Leverages machine learning, data analysis, and sometimes deep learning to make recommendations.

🤖
Robotics

Though not a subset of AI, robotics heavily relies on AI technologies. An interdisciplinary field combining mechanical engineering, electronics, computer science, and AI to create machines capable of performing tasks autonomously or semi-autonomously.

Note: Robotics is a distinct field, not a subset of AI

🗂️
Virtual Assistants

Computer programs that interact with humans in a 'natural' way. Assist users with tasks like scheduling appointments, making reservations, and providing information. Distinguished from chatbots — which are purely conversational — by broader task capability.

The AI Lifecycle
1
Problem Understanding & Risk

Define the problem, requirements, and constraints. Consider ethical implications, fairness, transparency, and privacy. Refer to NSW AI Assessment Framework.

2
Data Acquisition & Pre-processing

Identify and gather relevant data. Pre-process and clean it for quality and suitability. Comply with applicable laws and ethics.

3
ML Lifecycle Stages

Apply data pre-processing, feature engineering, model selection, training, evaluation, and deployment.

4
Additional AI Techniques

Consider NLP, computer vision, knowledge representation, inference, and reasoning methods beyond traditional ML.

5
Integration & System Design

Integrate ML models into a broader system or application architecture. Consider scalability, performance, and compatibility.

6
User Experience & Interaction

Design user interface components — chatbots, voice interfaces, or visualisations — for seamless user engagement.

7
Continuous Monitoring & Improvement

Implement mechanisms to monitor performance, gather user feedback, and iteratively improve based on new data or changing requirements.
