AI chatbots are coming to your workplace — not necessarily for your job

Kai Riemer & Sandra Peter · The Conversation · 2 Nov 2023

Conversational AI invites optimism about productivity, but responsible use starts with how LLMs work: probabilistic pattern engines, not omniscient brains — and a shift from job loss to job description and AI fluency.

Why context matters

Before workplace deployment, organisations need clarity on what chatbots are, how they produce text, and guardrails for use. Hype (e.g. a school “AI principal”) illustrates visibility — not substitute for governance.

Core limitation

LLMs do not hold knowledge like a database. At inference they do not access training data; each answer is generated anew from statistical patterns. Sounding authoritative ≠ being authoritative.

How chatbots work (in brief)

Large language models

  • Neural nets trained on vast text; training encodes language patterns in weights — “text as numbers / probabilities,” not a stored encyclopaedia.
  • Probabilistic, not deterministic — they cannot reliably serve as definitive factual sources; persuasion and genre mimicry follow linguistic regularities; isolated facts cannot be guaranteed from pattern completion alone.
  • When outputs align with reality, it is because knowledge is often embedded in language patterns, not because the model “knows” in a human sense.
  • Strictly speaking, generation is always open-ended; errors are often called hallucinations — the article stresses verifying outputs.

The “gifted grad student” mental model

Think of a chatbot as a capable graduate student assigned as your assistant: diligent on language tasks, but overconfident, prone to shortcuts, and able to produce answers that sound right without factual grounding. Verify outputs as you would a junior researcher’s draft. They do not comprehend questions with human understanding — yet they can augment language-heavy work when roles are chosen carefully.

ACE — three levels of capability

Assisting

A Summarise, analyse, refine text; extract themes and key points; restate arguments in clearer or more accessible language.

Creating

C Expand bullet points into reports or memos; shift genre and tone; adopt personas (strategist, scholar, marketer) to draft material for professional contexts.

Exploring

E Use as a sparring partner for hypotheticals; have it pose questions then answer; generate exemplars (e.g. a report or post) and reflect on what it did and why to build judgment.

Evidence from organisations (early)

Productivity snapshots — not universal proof

Illustrative cases: fine-tuned or embedded tools on internal data; gains vary by task and measurement.

Westpac (pilot) ~46% productivity gain in software coding tasks vs control; quality not reported as falling.
BCG experiment Consultants with chatbot support: +12.2% tasks completed, +25.1% speed, +40% quality vs baseline.
US software firm (reported) Proposal drafting using retrieval over large internal file sets — time savings on bid preparation.

Jobs vs job descriptions

Chatbots may not be “coming for your job” in a simple automation sense — probabilistic unreliability limits full hand-off — but they are coming for your job description: tasks and expectations will shift.

AI fluency — understanding how to work with these tools — is framed as becoming as basic as PC literacy once was.

Authorship & transparency

The authors state they did not let a chatbot write the article; they did use AI in preparation — analogised to using a computer rather than a typewriter. The Conversation expects disclosure of AI use in article preparation.
