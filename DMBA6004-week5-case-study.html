MST Framework Applied to Giant Swarm

Media Synchronicity Theory Conveyance Convergence

MST (Dennis & Valacich, 1999): effectiveness = matching medium to task. Conveyance (transmitting info) suits async. Convergence (building shared understanding) needs synchronous richness.

Key Tension

Giant Swarm nails conveyance — GitHub RFCs, 80/20 Slack rule, async deep work. But convergence is underserved: onboarding tacit knowledge, resolving conflict, aligning on complex architecture all need richer-media interaction.

Where it works

  • GitHub RFCs: ideal for complex technical conveyance — slow, considered, transparent
  • Slack coordination: 80/20 public rule creates accessible, searchable information trails

Where it fails at scale

  • Tacit onboarding: 1:1 calls can't scale to 100 employees; shared context doesn't transfer async
  • Synchronicity creep: Platform Architect (Exhibit 4) notes Slack is treated synchronously — non-European staff face implicit pressure to be online during European hours
  • Conflict & alignment: Text-based async amplifies misunderstanding; CAPI architectural decisions need faster convergence than RFC threads allow

// Frame Giant Swarm's communication strategy maps well onto Media Synchronicity Theory. Strong architecture, needs calibration for scale.

Anticipated Counterarguments

Likely Challenge Your Response
"GitHub/Slack notifications are synchronous enough." MST focuses on the task, not the tool. Even with notifications, async channels create latency in convergence. The Platform Architect's own quote confirms synchronicity creep — that's a symptom of tool-task mismatch, not a solution to it.
"The onsites provide the convergence they need." Onsites happen twice a year. MST requires convergence at the moment of ambiguity — not six months later. Trust built at onsites decays between events, especially for mid-cycle new hires.
"Documentation in GitHub IS tacit knowledge made explicit." Oliver Thylmann's own blog (Exhibit 10) says "moving tacit knowledge to documentation is futile." This is textbook MST — documentation handles conveyance, not convergence. They've already identified the limit.

Is Twice-a-Year Enough? The Glue Time Dependency Problem

Formalised Informal Communication Trust Decay Onsite Ritual

The onsite is a genuine cultural asset — concentrated bonding, cultural recalibration, signal of company values through action. But at 100+, trust must be continuous, not batch-processed.

Core Risk

A new hire joining in October may wait 6–8 months for their first onsite, integrating almost entirely through async text. The social foundation every "oldie" built over years of onsites doesn't transfer automatically.

Key risks of over-reliance

  • New hire trust deficit: No in-person experience for months; integration relies solely on async tools
  • Scaling economics: Flying 150+ people to a villa twice a year may become financially or logistically unviable
  • Access barriers: Caregiving responsibilities, health, or visa constraints make week-long events disproportionately hard for some staff

Formalised informal alternatives

  • Coffee Roulette (Donut.ai): Automated random pairing for 20-min informal video chats — serendipitous connection at ongoing cadence
  • Cohort-based onboarding: Group new hires into cohorts; replace the infeasible 1:1-with-everyone chain with structured peer social sessions
  • Team mini-onsites: Quarterly 8–12 person in-person meetups — cheaper, more accessible, sustains team-level cohesion between full-company events

Two Concrete Recommendations for Scaling the Giant Swarm Way

Rec 1: Culture Narration Rec 2: Situational Awareness Tacit Knowledge
Design Constraint

Every recommendation must amplify existing Giant Swarm culture — async, self-organised, transparent — not import foreign management structures.

Recommendation 1: Culture Narration ("Working Out Loud")

Invite tenured Swarmers to briefly narrate their decision-making in async short-form: weekly context dispatches explaining why a decision was made, a GitHub culture wiki built from concrete examples (not abstract rules), short Loom-style video walkthroughs of specific norms embedded in onboarding.

Oliver Thylmann (Exhibit 10) says tacit knowledge transfer via documentation is "futile" — but it CAN move through examples and story. This is the mechanism. Zero new tools, GitHub and Slack native, entirely opt-in.

Recommendation 2: Structured Situational Awareness

Address the remote awareness collapse that hits all scaling distributed teams. Three lightweight additions to existing infrastructure: a weekly "Swarm Digest" (partly auto-generated from GitHub/Slack activity) summarising cross-team decisions; a "Decision Trail" norm logging all RFC outcomes to a shared #decisions channel with context; an optional daily "working window" declaration for ambient work visibility.

This gives new hires the context to act autonomously sooner — consistent with the "Working with Adults" principle — without adding hierarchy or reporting overhead.

Rebuttal & Challenge Notes

Anticipated Counterarguments

Likely Challenge Your Response
"Culture can't be deliberately designed — it emerges organically." Giant Swarm already deliberately designs culture — the salary formula, buddy systems, onsites are all intentional infrastructure. The alternative, hoping it scales organically, is exactly what Anna warns against in Section 1.
"Culture Narration will create information overload." It's pull, not push — a dedicated channel new hires subscribe to at onboarding, not broadcast to everyone. The goal is a searchable body of cultural examples, not another firehose.
"A Swarm Digest sounds like a top-down management newsletter." It's collaboratively generated — partially automated from existing GitHub/Slack activity, partially crowd-sourced. A navigation aid for existing transparency, not a top-down broadcast.
