MST Framework Applied to Giant Swarm

Media Synchronicity Theory Conveyance Convergence Async-First

Core Theory Primer

Media Synchronicity Theory (Dennis & Valacich, 1999) argues that communication effectiveness depends on matching the medium's synchronicity to the communication task. Two key processes: conveyance (transmitting information, benefits from low synchronicity / async) and convergence (reaching shared understanding / agreement, benefits from high synchronicity).

Key Tension

Giant Swarm's default to async (Slack, GitHub) is theoretically ideal for conveyance. But as team size and distribution grow, convergence tasks — resolving ambiguity, building shared mental models, onboarding tacit knowledge — are harder to achieve asynchronously.

Where the Approach Excels (High MST Fit)

  • Technical documentation & RFC decisions: GitHub Issues/PRs are ideal low-synchronicity channels for transmitting complex information. The asynchronous nature allows deep, considered responses — pure conveyance tasks.
  • Routine team coordination: Slack channels like #afk, #team-X serve coordination needs without requiring real-time presence. The 80/20 public channel rule reinforces transparency and information accessibility (Exhibit 5).
  • Cross-timezone deep work: Async communication allows engineers across European time zones to contribute on their own schedule, avoiding the constant meeting overhead that would reduce heads-down coding time.
  • Accountability without surveillance: The "Adults" principle (Exhibit 4) is backed by written async trails — GitHub commits, Slack messages. This creates accountability without micromanagement — a clever MST-aligned design.

Where the Approach May Fail (MST Misalignment)

  • Onboarding new hires into tacit knowledge: Tacit knowledge (how we really do things here) requires convergence — shared, iterative dialogue. The 1:1 video calls in onboarding (Exhibit 7) partially address this, but as the company scales to 100+, the practice of scheduling calls with every employee becomes infeasible.
  • Cross-timezone synchronous bottlenecks: The Platform Architect (Exhibit 4) notes that heavy Slack reliance is "treated somewhat synchronously" — people expect fast replies. Staff in non-European time zones face implicit pressure to be online during European hours, violating the async premise.
  • Conflict resolution & sensitive feedback: Resolving interpersonal or cross-team disagreements requires high synchronicity (convergence). Text-based async channels can escalate misunderstandings, especially across cultural communication norms.
  • Strategic alignment and emergent decisions: Rapid iteration on complex architectural decisions (e.g., CAPI transition) may require synchronous convergence that GitHub RFCs can't efficiently provide. Comment threads can become unwieldy.

The Scaling Problem

At ~100 employees, the informal calibration that previously allowed async communication to "work" (shared context, existing social trust) weakens. MST predicts that as new employees arrive without shared context, convergence needs increase — but the infrastructure leans overwhelmingly async. Without deliberate convergence rituals at higher cadence, the risk is information overload (too many async threads) and miscommunication on ambiguous tasks.

Is Twice-a-Year Enough? The Glue Time Dependency Problem

Formalised Informal Communication Psychological Safety Trust Decay Onsite Ritual

What the Onsite Does Well

The bi-annual onsite (Exhibit 8) is a genuine cultural asset. It provides concentrated "Glue Time" — a term that implicitly acknowledges that remote-first work creates adhesion gaps. Key strengths:

  • Serendipitous interaction: Unstructured time generates informal knowledge sharing and social bonds that async tools cannot replicate. "Stories and shared experiences from onsites become part of the company's internal lore" (Exhibit 8).
  • Signal of commitment: The investment (renting villas, flying a global team, including families) signals that the company genuinely values human connection. This reinforces the "People-First" value through action, not words.
  • Cultural recalibration: Company-wide sessions on culture and founder updates serve as a reset point — a shared reference event that synchronises collective identity.
The Core Problem

At 100+ employees and growing, trust must be maintained continuously, not batch-processed. Trust built over a week in Mallorca decays over 6 months of purely virtual interaction — especially for employees hired mid-cycle who may not attend their first onsite for months.

Risks of Over-Reliance

  • New hire trust deficit: An employee joining in, say, October and missing the Spring onsite will operate for 6–8 months without the foundational in-person connection. Their integration into the high-trust culture relies almost entirely on async tools and the 1:1 call chain.
  • Scaling economics: Flying 100+ people to a European villa twice a year is expensive. At 150+ employees it may become logistically and financially impractical — or fragment into divisional onsites, which undermines the whole-company cohesion effect.
  • Trust decay between events: Research on distributed team cohesion suggests that without intermediate trust-maintenance touchpoints, team cohesion erodes measurably between intensive bonding events.
  • Diversity & inclusion risk: Employees with caregiving responsibilities, health constraints, or visa issues may find week-long away events disproportionately difficult. Family-inclusion policies help but don't fully resolve this.

Formalised Informal Communication: Practical Alternatives

  • Virtual Coffee Roulette: Automate random pairing of employees (via Slack bot) for 20-min informal video chats. Donut.ai (a Slack integration) is widely used for this. Creates the serendipitous cross-team connection that onsites provide, at ongoing cadence.
  • Team-Level Mini Onsites: Quarterly small-team (8–12 person) in-person meetups are more scalable than a full company gathering. These maintain the cohesion of individual teams and are more accessible logistically.
  • Structured Async Social Rituals: The #random and #chat-* channels exist but participation is voluntary. Consider structured weekly async rituals — e.g., "Photo Friday" (post a photo from your week), "Two Truths & a Lie" async threads — to maintain informal presence.
  • Onboarding Cohort Model: Rather than 1:1 calls with every employee (infeasible at scale), group new hires into cohorts and run structured social sessions. Creates peer bonds and reduces onboarding time investment.
  • "State of Me" Check-ins: The #metalistening channel (mentioned in Section 3) is a good seed. Formalise a lightweight monthly async "personal check-in" prompt that invites (not mandates) sharing of personal context, building ambient social awareness.

Two Concrete Recommendations for Scaling the Giant Swarm Way

Recommendation 1: Culture Narration Recommendation 2: Structured Situational Awareness Remote Integration Tacit Knowledge

Problem Framing

The core challenge in Sections 1, 3, and 6 is twofold: (1) implicit/tacit cultural knowledge doesn't transfer automatically to new hires, and (2) situational awareness — knowing what's happening, who's working on what, how decisions are made — degrades in remote environments as teams scale. The current onboarding model (Exhibit 7) was designed for a smaller company. Both problems are well-documented in the remote collaboration literature.

Guiding Principle

Recommendations must be concrete, actionable, and consistent with the "Giant Swarm Way" — they cannot introduce bureaucracy or undermine self-organisation. Every intervention should feel like an amplification of existing culture, not an imposition of new management.

Recommendation 1: The "Culture Narration" Practice

What: Establish a lightweight, structured practice where team members — especially the "oldies" — narrate their decision-making and work practices in short-form async content. Think of it as a "working out loud" ritual. This could take the form of:

  • Short weekly "context dispatches" posted to a dedicated Slack channel or GitHub repo — 200–300 words explaining why a decision was made, what norms were invoked, what was considered and rejected.
  • A "Culture Wiki" on GitHub (consistent with their GitHub-as-truth philosophy) where implicit norms are collaboratively documented not as rules but as examples: "Here's how we handled X, and here's why."
  • Video "norms walkthroughs" — 5-minute informal recordings by tenured staff on specific cultural practices (e.g., "how I use RFCs," "how I signal disagreement async") embedded in the onboarding guide.

Why it works: Oliver Thylmann himself noted (Exhibit 10) that tacit knowledge can't be moved to documentation directly — but it CAN be transferred through the exchange of examples and stories. This practice creates a continuous, searchable narration of culture that new hires can access asynchronously, at the moment they need it, in formats aligned with Giant Swarm's existing tools.

Fit with Giant Swarm Way: It's async, GitHub-native or Slack-native, voluntary but encouraged, and consistent with the transparency value. It doesn't require new tools or management approval gates.

Recommendation 2: Structured Situational Awareness Rituals

What: Introduce a lightweight cross-team situational awareness mechanism that gives new hires — and all employees — ambient visibility into what's happening across the organisation. Specifically:

  • "Swarm Digest" (weekly async newsletter, auto-generated + curated): A brief, structured summary of what each team shipped, what SIGs discussed, what decisions were made via RFC. Could be partially automated (GitHub activity summaries, Slack digest). Sent via a #news-company channel update. No new tool needed.
  • Personal "Working Window" declarations in Slack: Rather than just #afk, a lightweight daily async "here's what I'm working on today and why" practice. Builds ambient awareness of work context without requiring real-time presence. Opt-in, culture-normed rather than mandated.
  • Explicit "Decision Trails": For any significant decision made via RFC or in a team, a mandatory summary posted to a #decisions channel: decision taken, key context, who was involved, how to re-open. This prevents new hires from encountering inexplicable historical choices with no context.

Why it works: The situational awareness problem in remote work (documented extensively in the literature) is essentially a transparency-at-scale problem. Giant Swarm has transparency as a core value, but the mechanisms (open Slack channels, GitHub) work well when you have shared context. For new hires or cross-timezone employees, the firehose of information is unnavigable without curation. These rituals provide structured signals within the existing channel architecture.

Fit with Giant Swarm Way: Builds directly on existing tools and values. Reinforces transparency without adding hierarchy. Supports self-organisation by giving new hires the situational context to make autonomous decisions earlier.

"What about the diversity and inclusion problem mentioned in Section 6? Your recommendations don't address it." Fair point — our two recommendations are specifically scoped to the onboarding integration question asked.
