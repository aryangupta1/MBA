Question 01 · Media Synchronicity Theory
Evaluate Giant Swarm's communication strategy using Media Synchronicity Theory. Where does it work well — and where might it fail as the team grows?

② Script
③ Slide

Notes

Match the medium to the task

Conveyance = async ✓ Convergence = needs more

MST says communication falls into two types. Conveyance — sharing information — works well async. Convergence — getting everyone on the same page — needs richer, real-time interaction. Giant Swarm is great at conveyance. The gap is convergence.

The Core Gap

At 100+ people, new hires don't yet have the shared context that makes async work. Without it, getting aligned — on culture, decisions, disagreements — is much harder over Slack threads.

Where it works

  • GitHub RFCs — people can think before responding. Good for complex decisions.
  • 80/20 Slack rule — keeps information public and searchable for everyone.
  • Async default — engineers get uninterrupted time to do deep work.

Where it breaks

  • Onboarding — you can't absorb the unwritten cultural rules through text alone.
  • Time zone pressure — a Platform Architect noted Slack gets treated as synchronous. Non-European staff feel left behind.
  • Conflict and alignment — tricky conversations need tone and nuance that text can't carry.

Rebuttal

If someone pushes back…

Challenge Your response
"Slack notifications make it fast enough." Speed isn't the issue — richness is. Notifications don't give you tone or nuance. A Platform Architect literally said Slack gets used synchronously already — that's a signal the tool isn't quite right for the way people are using it.
"The onsites cover the convergence needs." Convergence needs to happen in the moment — when a decision is live, or a new hire is confused. Waiting six months for an onsite doesn't help then.
"GitHub documentation handles tacit knowledge." Even Oliver Thylmann says you can't capture tacit knowledge in documentation — it moves through stories and examples. The case itself backs this up.
Question 02 · High-Trust Culture & Rituals
Is the bi-annual onsite enough to maintain a high-trust culture at 100+ people? What are the risks — and what other strategies could help?
① Notes
② Script
③ Slide
④ Rebuttal
Notes

The onsite is great — but trust can't wait six months

Necessary ✓ Not sufficient on its own

The onsite is one of Giant Swarm's best ideas. A week together, shared meals, no agenda — it builds real bonds that carry over into months of remote work. But trust needs to be maintained continuously, not topped up twice a year.

The Problem

Someone joining in October might not go to their first onsite until April or May. That's five or six months integrating mostly through text. By the time they arrive, the "oldies" have already formed their bonds.

Three real risks

  • New hires feel isolated — months of async work before any real connection
  • Doesn't scale financially — flying 150+ people to a villa twice a year gets expensive fast
  • Not everyone can attend — caregivers, health issues, visa constraints

Simple ways to fill the gap

  • Coffee Roulette — a Slack bot randomly pairs people for a quick 20-min casual chat. Zero setup, ongoing connection.
  • Onboarding cohorts — group new starters together instead of 99 individual 1:1 calls each
  • Team mini-onsites — 8 to 10 people, a day or two, quarterly. Cheap and easy to organise.

It just needs backup — smaller, more frequent moments that keep trust topped up between the big events.

Rebuttal

If someone pushes back…

Challenge Your response
"It's worked for 10 years — why change it?" The case says it's already struggling. Anna directly describes onboarding as "getting harder." It worked for 60 people. The question is whether it still works at 100.
"Coffee chats feel forced and people won't use them." They're completely opt-in. Giant Swarmers already voluntarily chat in #random and interest channels — the appetite is there. We're just giving it a little nudge.
"The current onboarding already covers new hires." It was designed for a team of 40. At 100 employees, doing a 1:1 call with every single person isn't practical. A cohort model gives the same result with a fraction of the effort.
Question 03 · Scaling the Culture
What are your top two concrete recommendations to help new employees successfully integrate into the "Giant Swarm Way"?

② Script

Notes

Two recommendations that feel like Giant Swarm

Rec 1: Work Out Loud Rec 2: Weekly Digest No new tools. No new hierarchy.
The rule

If it could come from a corporate HR playbook, it's the wrong answer. Every recommendation has to feel async, self-organised, and transparent — just like Giant Swarm already is.

Recommendation 1 — Work Out Loud

Ask experienced Swarmers to occasionally share why they made a decision, not just what they decided. A short Slack post, a quick GitHub note, a two-minute video. Over time, this builds a real library of how the culture actually works — in real examples, not an abstract handbook.

Oliver Thylmann says tacit knowledge travels through stories, not documentation. This is that — just made a little more intentional.

Recommendation 2 — Weekly Swarm Digest

A short weekly summary of what happened across the company — decisions made, things shipped, RFCs closed. Partly automated from GitHub and Slack activity. New hires go from drowning in a firehose to having a readable map of what matters.

This isn't management broadcasting to staff. It's the team's own activity, organised so it's actually usable.

Rebuttal

If someone pushes back…

Challenge Your response
"Culture grows naturally — you can't engineer it." Giant Swarm already engineers it — the salary formula, buddy systems, onsites are all deliberate. The question is whether the design keeps up as the company grows. Anna says right now it isn't.
"Work Out Loud will just create more Slack noise." It goes in its own dedicated channel that people subscribe to — not pushed to everyone. New hires look it up when they need it. It's pull, not push.
"A digest sounds like a top-down company newsletter." It's generated from what's already happening in public GitHub repos and Slack channels — not written by management. It's curation, not broadcasting. The content is already there; we're just organising it.
