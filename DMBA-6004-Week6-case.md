# Question 1

> Using Erin Meyer’s framework, how would you classify Giant Swarm’s dominant leadership and decision-making culture? Is it Egalitarian/Consensual, Egalitarian/Top-down, or something else? Justify your answer with evidence from the case.

## Notes

* Giant Swarm is best classified as **primarily Egalitarian/Consensual**
* The strongest evidence is its **minimal hierarchy**, **self-organisation**, and strong expectation that people **speak up and contribute**
* The company operates with a **flat-ish structure**, where authority is not heavily concentrated in formal managers
* The culture is built around **trust, transparency, and autonomy**, shown through the idea of “working with adults”
* Employees are expected to:

  * manage themselves
  * make decisions responsibly
  * raise issues and contribute ideas openly
* Decision-making appears to be **discussion-led rather than purely directive**
* Teams, chapters, and SIGs create a structure where input is gathered across the organisation rather than simply passed down from above
* The use of RFC-style processes and asynchronous discussion also suggests a **consultative and consensus-building approach**
* However, Giant Swarm is **not purely consensual in an idealistic sense**
* Because it is a fast-moving remote tech company, there is still a need for:

  * speed
  * pragmatism
  * individual initiative
* This means its culture is better described as:

  * **dominantly Egalitarian/Consensual**
  * with some **flexible execution that can look more Egalitarian/Top-down in practice**
* The key point is that authority is relatively flat, but decisions are still expected to be made responsibly and efficiently, not discussed forever

## Script

Giant Swarm’s dominant leadership and decision-making culture is best classified as primarily egalitarian and consensual. The case shows that the company has minimal hierarchy, values self-organisation, and expects employees to speak up, take responsibility, and act like trusted adults. That strongly aligns with an egalitarian culture because authority is not based heavily on rank or formal management layers.

It also leans strongly toward consensual decision-making because teams are expected to contribute input, discussions happen openly, and knowledge-sharing structures like teams, chapters, SIGs, and RFC-style processes all support consultation before action. So decisions are shaped collectively rather than simply handed down.

That said, I would not call Giant Swarm purely egalitarian-consensual in an absolute sense. Because it is a remote-first tech company operating in a complex and fast-moving environment, it also needs speed and initiative. So in practice, some decisions may still happen more quickly once enough input has been gathered. Overall, though, the dominant culture is clearly egalitarian and consensual, with some pragmatic flexibility in execution.

## Slide content

* Primarily **Egalitarian + Consensual**

**Why**

* Minimal hierarchy
* Strong self-organisation
* “Working with adults” mindset
* Speak-up culture
* Team input shapes decisions

**Nuance**

* Not purely consensus-driven
* Fast-moving environment requires flexibility

**Conclusion**

* Dominantly egalitarian/consensual
* Pragmatic execution when needed

# Question 2

> Imagine Giant Swarm hires two new senior engineers: one from Japan (typically Hierarchical/Consensual) and one from the USA (typically Egalitarian/Top-down). What specific misunderstandings or challenges might arise for each of them within Giant Swarm’s existing culture? How could Giant Swarm’s onboarding process be adapted to address these potential cultural frictions?

## Notes

* The Japanese engineer may struggle because Giant Swarm expects:

  * autonomy
  * visible initiative
  * open participation
* In a more hierarchical and consensual background, they may expect:

  * clearer authority
  * more guidance
  * more careful alignment before speaking or acting
* They may hesitate to challenge ideas publicly or act without clearer direction
* Their caution could be misread as:

  * passivity
  * low confidence
  * lack of engagement
* The US engineer may struggle for the opposite reason
* Coming from a more egalitarian and top-down style, they may expect:

  * open discussion at first
  * then a faster final decision
  * clearer decision ownership
* They may see Giant Swarm’s collaborative and discussion-heavy style as:

  * slow
  * vague
  * inefficient
* They may push for action before others feel alignment has been reached
* Their directness could be perceived as:

  * impatience
  * dominance
  * not respecting the collaborative process
* The onboarding issue is that Giant Swarm’s culture relies heavily on norms that may feel obvious to insiders but unclear to newcomers
* Onboarding should not just introduce tools and people, but also explain:

  * how decisions are made
  * what “speaking up” actually looks like
  * when autonomy is expected
  * how disagreement is handled
* Useful onboarding improvements:

  * clearer explanation of decision-making norms
  * examples of how successful employees operate in the culture
  * buddy support focused on cultural navigation, not just admin
  * explicit explanation that autonomy does not mean isolation
  * explicit explanation that consultation does not mean indecision
* Main insight:

  * the challenge is not technical skill
  * it is mismatch in assumptions about hierarchy, voice, and decision speed

## Script

For the Japanese engineer, the biggest challenge would likely be Giant Swarm’s expectation of visible autonomy and open participation. Someone from a more hierarchical and consensual background may expect clearer authority, more guidance, and more careful alignment before speaking up or acting. In Giant Swarm, that could create hesitation, and their behaviour might be misread as low initiative when it is actually cultural caution.

For the US engineer, the likely challenge is the opposite. They may be comfortable speaking up and operating in a flat structure, but they may expect faster decision-making and clearer ownership once discussion begins. Giant Swarm’s collaborative and alignment-oriented style could feel slower or less defined than expected, and they may push for action before others feel ready.

So the onboarding process should be adapted to make these norms explicit. Giant Swarm should clearly explain how decisions are made, what kind of participation is expected, how autonomy works, and how disagreement is handled. A buddy system should also help translate the culture in practice, not just help with logistics. The main point is that cultural friction here comes less from capability and more from different assumptions about authority, communication, and decision-making.

## Slide content

**Potential cultural frictions**

**Japanese engineer**

* May expect clearer authority
* More cautious about speaking up
* Autonomy may feel ambiguous

**US engineer**

* May expect faster decisions
* May push action earlier
* Could see consensus as slow

**Onboarding fixes**

* Explain decision-making norms clearly
* Show what “speaking up” looks like
* Use buddy support for cultural adjustment
* Make autonomy and consultation explicit

# Question 3

> Giant Swarm’s culture is described as having “minimal hierarchy” and valuing “speaking up.” How might this approach need to be intentionally managed or communicated differently to be effective and inclusive for employees from cultures with high Power Distance (as per Hofstede) or a more hierarchical background?

## Notes

* Giant Swarm’s culture assumes that speaking up is natural, positive, and expected
* For employees from high power distance or more hierarchical cultures, speaking up may not feel natural at all
* They may associate authority with:

  * respect
  * deference
  * not challenging publicly
* This means a flat culture can unintentionally advantage people who are already comfortable with direct participation
* Risk:

  * silence gets misread as disengagement or lack of ideas
  * confident voices dominate
  * inclusion becomes uneven even if the structure looks open
* “Minimal hierarchy” does not automatically create psychological safety
* It removes formal barriers, but cultural barriers may still remain
* To be inclusive, Giant Swarm must make its expectations explicit rather than assumed
* Employees need to be told clearly:

  * that participation is valued
  * that respectful disagreement is acceptable
  * how to challenge ideas constructively
* Leaders need to do more than just leave the floor open
* They need to actively create participation by:

  * inviting quieter people in
  * using structured turn-taking
  * allowing written or async input
  * checking understanding directly
* Written and asynchronous channels may be especially useful because they lower the pressure of immediate verbal challenge
* The company should frame speaking up not as confrontation, but as contribution and care for the work
* Main insight:

  * inclusion in a flat culture requires intentional design
  * otherwise “openness” mainly benefits those already culturally comfortable with openness

## Script

Giant Swarm’s minimal hierarchy and speak-up culture sound very inclusive on the surface, but they do not automatically work for everyone in the same way. For employees from high power distance or more hierarchical backgrounds, speaking up may feel uncomfortable because respect is often shown through deference, not open challenge. So if Giant Swarm simply assumes everyone will contribute in the same way, silence may be misread as disengagement rather than a cultural difference.

To make this culture more effective and inclusive, Giant Swarm needs to communicate its norms much more explicitly. It should clearly explain that respectful disagreement is valued, what good participation looks like, and how people are expected to contribute. Leaders also need to actively create space for input rather than assuming an open floor is enough. That could include inviting quieter people directly, using async channels, and structuring discussions so contribution does not depend only on confidence. The key point is that flat culture is not automatically inclusive. It has to be intentionally supported if Giant Swarm wants employees from different cultural backgrounds to participate fully.

## Slide content

**Making “minimal hierarchy” inclusive**

**Challenge**

* Speaking up is not culturally natural for everyone
* Silence may be misread

**Risk**

* Confident voices dominate
* Flat culture feels open, but not equally inclusive

**What Giant Swarm should do**

* Make norms explicit
* Define respectful disagreement
* Invite input actively
* Use async and structured participation

**Conclusion**

* Flat culture needs intentional inclusion design
