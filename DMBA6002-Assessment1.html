Murray–Darling Basin · Murrumbidgee Catchment
The project
One Basin CRC — Anomaly Detection & Health Prediction
AI system using machine learning on sensor and telemetry data to predict and diagnose failures in gravity-fed irrigation infrastructure — before they cause delivery outages.
The catchment
Murrumbidgee, Murray–Darling Basin
676,000 ha gravity-fed network · 2,200+ irrigators · Burrinjuck & Blowering Dams · Operated by Murrumbidgee Irrigation (named CRC partner).
The core problem
Infrastructure fragility — not water scarcity
Gate faults, sensor drift, telemetry dropouts, and power failures cause delivery failures the current system cannot predict. This is an operational reliability problem, not a policy one.

Stakeholder map — who is affected and how
Primary — operational
Murrumbidgee Irrigation WaterNSW NSW water agencies
Run the assets the AI monitors. MI is the named CRC project partner — simultaneously the beneficiary of the technology and the organisation responsible for implementing it on the ground.
Primary — affected
Irrigators (2,200+) Env. water holders MLDRIN Griffith community
Bear the downstream consequences of failures. Most exposed to risk, least formal power over how the AI system is designed or governed.
Secondary & tertiary
MDBA NSW Farmers One Basin CRC CSIRO
Set policy rules, fund research, advocate for communities. Shape the regulatory and political environment within which AI-assisted irrigation tools must prove their legitimacy.
What each stakeholder needs from the AI system
Infrastructure operators (MI, WaterNSW)
  • Explainable, confidence-scored alerts — not raw model outputs
  • Low false positive rate (too many false alarms = operators stop trusting the system)
  • Seamless SCADA integration, no specialist data-science skill required
  • Field-usable in real delivery decisions, in real time
Irrigators
  • Advance notice of disruptions — recovery is impossible within the same season
  • Transparency: no black-box decisions about their water access
  • A clear process to contest AI-driven outcomes
  • Procedural fairness — consistent rules regardless of farm size or location
Environmental water managers
  • Precise delivery timing — not just volume; a week late can miss an ecological window entirely
  • Hydrographs maintained during sensitive ecological periods
  • Infrastructure faults caught before they delay environmental flows
First Nations — MLDRIN
  • Cultural flows protected — not overridden by AI delivery prioritisation
  • Data sovereignty over catchment data the system collects
  • Co-governance in design, not late-stage consultation
  • Recognition that the AI operates on Country, not in a governance vacuum
Key risks and tensions — what could go wrong
01
Equity & fairness risk
AI trained on well-monitored nodes underperforms at tail-end, data-sparse areas. Existing infrastructure inequalities become embedded in algorithmic outputs — harder to contest than an explicit policy decision.
02
Accountability gap
When the AI misses a fault or triggers a false response — who is liable? The operator who acted? MI? The CRC researchers? No accountability framework currently exists for AI-driven water delivery decisions.
03
Trust & transparency
Without explainability, audit trails, and contestability, even a technically accurate model will be resisted by the people it governs. Trust must be built through process, not assumed from performance.
Core argument
This is not an engineering upgrade with governance implications — it is a governance intervention with an engineering component. It redistributes operational risk, creates new data dependencies, and alters decision-making authority across the Murrumbidgee system. Its success depends as much on stakeholder trust, explainability, and co-design as on model accuracy. Treating it as purely technical is the most predictable failure mode.
Theory: Clarke & Michael (2022) — multi-stakeholder risk in socio-technical interventions. Distinguishes formal power from operational power and exposure to risk.
Systems lens: Cosijn et al. (2023) — iceberg model, joint optimisation, feedback loops. Events → patterns → structures → mental models.
Policy context: MDBA 2026 Basin Plan Review — live consultation open now, closes May 2026. Ageing infrastructure and AI adoption are live review issues.
