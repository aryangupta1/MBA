Early Schools of Thought
Approach 01
Symbolic Logic & Expert Systems

Some researchers favoured systems combining facts about the world with axioms — like those of geometry and symbolic logic — to infer appropriate responses. By the 1980s this had become mainstream: "expert systems" using symbolic logic captured and applied human know-how. Japan invested heavily in this direction. But these systems proved too inflexible for the messiness of the real world, triggering an AI winter by the late 1980s.

Approach 02
Probabilistic / Neural Networks

Others preferred systems where the probability of one thing depended on constantly updated probabilities of many others — inspired by how biological neurons work. These artificial neural networks learn by exposure to examples, with connection "weights" adjusted until a given input produces an appropriate output. Though initially slow, they would eventually triumph with the help of GPU hardware.

Chronological Timeline
1940s
Origins
Neurons, Turing & Von Neumann

As the rudiments of how brain cells (neurons) work were pieced together in the 1940s, computer scientists began wondering if machines could be wired up the same way. Alan Turing — for whom the Turing Award is named — wondered about thinking machines, as did John von Neumann, who would later inspire McCarthy. Marvin Minsky made the first attempt to model neural networks in hardware.

Alan Turing · John von Neumann · Marvin Minsky
1956
Founding Moment
The Dartmouth Conference

A small but illustrious group gathered at Dartmouth College, New Hampshire, called together by young researcher John McCarthy. The group included Claude Shannon (begetter of information theory) and Herb Simon (the only person to win both the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences and the Turing Award). McCarthy wanted to discuss "how to make machines use language, form abstractions and concepts" and "solve kinds of problems now reserved for humans." He coined the term artificial intelligence — broad enough to encompass all existing approaches.

John McCarthy · Claude Shannon · Herb Simon
1980s
Consensus & Collapse
Expert Systems & the First AI Winter

After decades of intellectual ferment, the field reached wide agreement on "expert systems" — using symbolic logic to capture and apply human know-how. The Japanese government threw its weight behind the idea, investing in both the systems and the hardware they might need. But the systems proved too inflexible to cope with the messiness of the real world. By the late 1980s, AI had fallen into disrepute — a byword for overpromising and underdelivering. Researchers started to shun the term entirely.

Expert Systems · AI Winter · Japan's Fifth Generation Project
Early 1990s
Quiet Persistence
Neural Nets Sort the Post

From pockets of perseverance, the neural network approach survived. By the early 1990s, neural networks had been trained to recognise handwritten numbers — useful for sorting post. Researchers believed adding more layers of neurons might allow more sophisticated achievements, but this also made systems run much more slowly, limiting progress.

Handwriting Recognition · Multi-layer Networks
2009
Hardware Revolution
The GPU Breakthrough — 70× Speed-Up

Researchers at Stanford University dramatically demonstrated GPU potential by increasing neural net speed 70-fold using a gaming PC in their dorm room. The key: alongside the standard CPU, the gaming PC had a Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) — designed to create game worlds — which happened to be perfectly suited to running neural-network code. Coupling this hardware speed-up with more efficient training algorithms meant networks with millions of connections could now be trained in a reasonable time, enabling deeper, more capable architectures.

GPU · Stanford · Deep Learning Hardware
2012
Breakthrough
ImageNet & the Deep Learning Revolution

The power of deep learning became apparent at the ImageNet Challenge. Systems were trained on a database of more than a million labelled images (several hundred photos per word category) and challenged to label unseen test images. A team led by Geoff Hinton at the University of Toronto used deep learning to achieve 85% accuracy — instantly recognised as a breakthrough. Deep learning was also applied to speech recognition (mapping sound to text), face recognition (mapping faces to names), and translation.

Geoff Hinton · ImageNet · University of Toronto
2015
Dominance
Deep Learning Surpasses Human Performance

By 2015, almost everyone in the image-recognition field was using deep learning. The winning accuracy at the ImageNet Challenge had reached 96% — better than the average human score. Consumer products proliferated: Amazon's Alexa, online transcription services, and automatic web browser translations. Saying something was "AI-powered" became cool rather than embarrassing — though nearly every such product actually relied on deep learning under the hood.

Amazon Alexa · 96% Accuracy · Consumer AI
2017
Architecture Shift
The Transformer

A qualitative change was added to the quantitative gains of more computing power and data: a new way of arranging connections between neurons called the transformer. Transformers enable neural networks to track patterns in input even when elements of the pattern are far apart, allowing them to bestow "attention" on particular features in data. This gave networks a better grasp of context, enabling a technique called self-supervised learning: words are randomly blanked during training, and the model teaches itself to fill in the most likely candidate — allowing training on billions of words of raw internet text without any human labelling.

Transformer · Attention Mechanism · Self-Supervised Learning
2019
Large Language Models
GPT-2 & Emergent Behaviour

Transformer-based Large Language Models (LLMs) began attracting wider attention in 2019 when OpenAI released GPT-2 (Generative Pre-trained Transformer). LLMs turned out to be capable of "emergent" behaviour for which they had not been explicitly trained. Soaking up huge amounts of language made them not just adept at linguistic tasks like summarisation or translation, but also at things implicit in the training data — like simple arithmetic and writing software. Less happily, they also reproduced biases and prejudices in the data fed to them.

OpenAI · GPT-2 · Emergent Behaviour · Bias
Nov 2022
Consumer Revolution
ChatGPT: The Fastest-Growing Consumer Product Ever

A larger OpenAI model, GPT-3.5, was presented to the public as a chatbot. Anyone with a web browser could enter a prompt and get a response. No consumer product has ever taken off quicker. Within weeks, ChatGPT was generating everything from college essays to computer code. The shift: where the first cohort of AI-powered products was based on recognition, this second one is based on generation. Other generative models — Stable Diffusion and DALL-E using diffusion to turn text prompts into images — debuted around the same time. Rivals include Gemini (Google) and Claude (Anthropic, founded by researchers previously at OpenAI).

ChatGPT · GPT-3.5 · DALL-E · Stable Diffusion · Gemini · Claude
Key Concepts Explained
🧠
Artificial Neural Networks

Layers of interconnected simulated neurons. Not programmed with explicit rules — instead they "learn" by being exposed to lots of examples. During training, the strength of connections (called "weights") are repeatedly adjusted so that a given input eventually produces an appropriate output.

📐
Deep Learning

Neural networks with many layers ("deeper" networks). More layers allow more sophisticated pattern recognition. The deeper the network and the more training data, the better the performance. Became the dominant paradigm after 2012's ImageNet breakthrough.

⚡
GPU (Graphics Processing Unit)

Originally designed to render game worlds on screen, GPUs turned out to be ideally suited to running neural-network code. A Stanford dorm-room experiment in 2009 showed they could speed up neural nets 70-fold compared to standard CPUs — unlocking the deep learning era.

🔄
The Transformer

A new way (introduced 2017) of arranging neuron connections that lets networks keep track of patterns even when relevant elements are far apart — bestowing "attention" on particular features. Transformers underpin all modern large language models.

📝
Self-Supervised Learning

During training, some words are randomly blanked out, and the model teaches itself to fill in the most likely candidate. Because training data doesn't need to be labelled in advance, models can train on billions of words of raw internet text — vastly scaling up what they can learn.

✨
Emergent Behaviour

Capabilities that LLMs exhibit which they were not explicitly trained for. Absorbing huge amounts of language made GPT-2 and its successors adept not just at linguistic tasks, but also at arithmetic and coding — skills that were merely implicit in the training data.

🌊
Diffusion Models

A technique used by models like Stable Diffusion and DALL-E to turn text prompts into images — and also to generate video, speech, and music. Part of the second wave of AI products, focused on generation rather than recognition.

🏛️
Expert Systems

The 1980s consensus approach: using symbolic logic to capture and apply the best of human know-how in a specific domain. Backed heavily by Japan. Ultimately proved too rigid for real-world messiness, contributing to the AI winter of the late 1980s.

Alan Turing
Pioneer · 1940s

Wondered about machines that could think like people. The Turing Award — computing's highest honour — is named for him.

John von Neumann
Pioneer · 1940s

Theoretical inspiration to John McCarthy and the broader field of computing and AI.

John McCarthy
Founder of AI · 1956

Called together the Dartmouth Conference and coined the term "artificial intelligence" — broad enough to encompass all existing approaches.

Claude Shannon
Information Theory · 1956

Begetter of information theory; attended the Dartmouth Conference as one of its most illustrious participants.

Herb Simon
Nobel & Turing Laureate · 1956

The only person ever to win both the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences and the Turing Award. Dartmouth attendee.

Marvin Minsky
Neural Networks · 1940s–

Made the first attempt to model neural networks in hardware. Later abandoned the idea, though others took it forward. Attended the Dartmouth Conference.

Geoff Hinton
Deep Learning · 2012

Then at the University of Toronto; led the team that used deep learning to achieve 85% accuracy at the 2012 ImageNet Challenge — instantly recognised as a landmark breakthrough.

Product Milestones
Early 1990s
Handwritten Digit Recognition

Neural networks trained to help sort post by recognising handwritten numbers — an early practical demonstration of the technology's usefulness.

~2015
Amazon Alexa

Voice-driven devices powered by deep learning speech recognition appeared in consumers' homes, making AI feel useful and everyday.

~2015
Online Transcription & Translation

Online transcription services became genuinely useful; web browsers began offering automatic translation — powered entirely by deep learning.

2022
DALL-E & Stable Diffusion

Generative image models using diffusion debuted, turning text prompts into images. Other models followed capable of producing realistic video, speech, and music.

Nov 2022
ChatGPT

GPT-3.5 launched as a public chatbot. No consumer product had ever grown faster. Within weeks it was generating college essays and computer code.

Post-2022
Gemini & Claude

Rivals emerged: Gemini from Google and Claude from Anthropic — founded by researchers previously at OpenAI — expanding the generative AI ecosystem.
